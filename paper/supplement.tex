\section{Implementation details of the verifier}
\label{sec:supp-verifier}

The pairwise overlap matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is computed in
fully vectorized form:
\begin{verbatim}
  # centers: (N, 2), sizes: (N, 2)
  cx, cy = centers[:, 0], centers[:, 1]
  sw, sh = sizes[:, 0], sizes[:, 1]
  ovx = torch.clamp_min(
      torch.minimum(cx[:,None]+sw[:,None]/2, cx[None,:]+sw[None,:]/2)
    - torch.maximum(cx[:,None]-sw[:,None]/2, cx[None,:]-sw[None,:]/2), 0)
  ovy = torch.clamp_min(
      torch.minimum(cy[:,None]+sh[:,None]/2, cy[None,:]+sh[None,:]/2)
    - torch.maximum(cy[:,None]-sh[:,None]/2, cy[None,:]-sh[None,:]/2), 0)
  A = ovx * ovy
  A.fill_diagonal_(0)
\end{verbatim}
Boundary violation along axis $a \in \{x,y\}$ and edge $e \in \{-,+\}$:
$b_i^{a,-} = \max(0,\, s_i^a/2 - c_i^a)$,
$b_i^{a,+} = \max(0,\, c_i^a + s_i^a/2 - W_a)$, with
$b_i = \sum_{a,e} b_i^{a,e}$.

repulsive$+$attractive forces on these drafts because every macro overlaps a

\section{LOCO cross-validation for $\lambda$}
\label{sec:supp-lambda-loco}

To answer the reviewer suggestion ``include a principled validation
protocol for choosing $\lambda$,'' we run leave-one-circuit-out
cross-validation on the $\lambda$-sweep results: for each held-out
circuit, choose $\hat\lambda$ that maximises the strict-Pareto count on
the other 5 circuits (tie-break by Pareto-improvement score, then by
smaller $\lambda$), then evaluate on the held-out circuit.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\input{figures/table_lambda_loco}
\caption{LOCO cross-validation for $\lambda$ selection on the
$\lambda$-sweep results.  $\hat\lambda$ is chosen on the 5 held-in
circuits (objective: strict-Pareto count, tie-break smaller $\lambda$);
$\Delta v$ and $\Delta h$ are evaluated on the held-out circuit.

\section{Independent classical force-directed legalizer}
\label{sec:supp-classical}

To address the reviewer concern about strong-baseline coverage
beyond ML-only baselines (cg / RePaint), we implement an independent
classical force-directed legalizer that does \emph{not} use VSR's
selector, severity, or net-spring.  All operators run for $T{=}100$
steps with step size $0.3$ on the same 15 cached ChipDiffusion drafts
(adaptec1$\times 8$ + adaptec2$\times 5$ + adaptec3$\times 1$ +
bigblue1$\times 1$, the union of currently-cached drafts).

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{FD-pure}: pairwise overlap repulsion + boundary force,
all macros free.  This is the standard analytical force-directed
legalizer reduction.
\item \textbf{FD+spring}: FD-pure + a degree-normalised
net-spring attraction with weight $1.0$ on every netlist edge,
NTUplace3 / RePLACE-flavoured.
\item \textbf{VSR-post (ours)}: selector mask + repulsion + boundary
+ degree-normalised net-spring with $\lambda{=}2$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Tuned baselines: sweeps of cg legality weight and RePaint $t_\text{start}$}
\label{sec:supp-tuned-baselines}

A natural concern is that the cg-strong and RePaint baselines used in the main
paper might be undertuned.  We address this with a per-circuit hyperparameter
sweep: cg legality weight $w \in \{0.5, 1, 2, 4, 8\}$ (with HPWL
guidance set to zero, the strongest legality regime) and RePaint

violating macros.  All 6 circuits at seed $42$, same backbone, $T=100$
denoising steps; this is exactly the setup of main paper Table~1.
For each (family, circuit) we pick the hp that minimises post-method
violation count and report it against the corresponding VSR-post
($\lambda{=}2$) result.  Table~\ref{tab:supp-tuned-baselines}.

This is the strongest guarantee we can offer that the comparison in
Table~1 is not a tuning artifact: even at \emph{per-circuit best}
settings, neither cg nor RePaint dominates VSR-post on the violation
axis.  The asymmetry is intuitive --- cg pushes against pairwise overlap
gradients without acknowledging severity, and RePaint with a binary mask
re-noises the violating macros without preserving their context, so the
re-sampled draft frequently lands in a worse basin than the original.

\section{Full-design HPWL with cells fixed at \texttt{.pl} positions}
\label{sec:supp-full-hpwl}

The main paper reports HPWL on the macro subgraph (macro-to-macro nets
only), as is standard in macro-placement literature.  A reviewer
correctly pointed out that this excludes any wirelength change between
macros and standard cells.  We re-run all 24 (circuit, seed) trials and
record the full-design HPWL: each macro is placed by the indicated
method, all standard cells are held fixed at their industrial
\texttt{.pl} positions, and HPWL is computed on the full netlist.
Table~\ref{tab:supp-full-hpwl}.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\input{figures/table_full_metrics}
\caption{Full per-trial metrics across 24 (circuit, seed) trials,
$\lambda{=}2$ post-VSR.  $\Delta\text{oa}\,\%$ is the change in total
overlap area $\sum_{ij} A_{ij}/2$ (a continuous quantity sensitive to
near-misses, in addition to the integer violation count).
$\Delta h_\text{macro}\,\%$ is the macros-only HPWL (matches main-paper
Table~1).  $\Delta h_\text{full}\,\%$ is the full-design HPWL with cells
held at \texttt{.pl} positions.  Two findings: (i) the full-design
HPWL median ($+10.7\%$) is comparable to the macros-only median
($+8.7\%$), so the macro-axis HPWL trend generalises to the full design
on most circuits; (ii) on adaptec2 the full-design HPWL is $-31\%$
(macros-only is $+9\%$), suggesting that VSR's repair on adaptec2 moves
macros into positions that better match the cell distribution; (iii) on
adaptec3 the full-design HPWL inflates more sharply than macros-only
(consistent with adaptec3 having dense local cell-macro coupling that
VSR does not reason about).  VSR-intra-soft is HPWL-neutral on the full
design (median $-1.6\%$).  Raw rows:
\texttt{results/vsr\_extra/full\_metrics.json}.}
\label{tab:supp-full-hpwl}
\end{table}

We treat this as honest evidence rather than uniformly-good news: VSR's
HPWL-preservation property does extend to the full netlist on most
circuits, but not on adaptec3, where dense local cell-macro coupling
penalises any macro displacement.  A future version of the operator
that reads the cell distribution as additional context would address
this.

\section{Neural repair operator: full training details}
\label{sec:supp-neural}

The negative-result GNN repair operator referenced in \S5 of the main paper
spans six training regimes:

\paragraph{Architecture.} 3-layer GATv2 (heads$=4$, hidden$=128$, $\sim 100$\,K
parameters), scale-invariant input normalization (centers and severity divided
by $\max(W, H)$).  Node features:
$[\mathrm{severity}, \mathrm{boundary}, \mathrm{n\_overlappers}, s^w, s^h]$.
Edge features: 4-dim pin offsets from ChipDiffusion's \texttt{edge\_attr}.

\paragraph{Loss.} L2 on the predicted displacement against a target defined
by the variant.

\paragraph{Variants.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Synthetic teacher}: hand-crafted operator outputs on synthetic
graphs (2500 train, 200 val).
\item \textbf{Real teacher}: hand-crafted on diffusion-sampled ISPD2005 placements
(3 samples $\times$ up to 3 circuits = 3--9 samples).
\item \textbf{Data augmentation}: per-sample $\times 50$ random perturbations.
\item \textbf{Trajectory distillation}: $(x_k, x_{k+1})$ pairs from every
hand-crafted step; 30 steps $\times$ 3 samples $=$ 90 pairs.
\item \textbf{Ground-truth target}: ISPD2005 industrial \texttt{.pl} placements.
\item \textbf{Residual learning}: predict $x_{GT} - x_{\text{hand}}$ after
hand-crafted convergence.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Findings.}  Best validation L2 was $0.0037$ (trajectory
distillation), but at inference on ISPD2005 the best variant (residual
$s{=}0.1$) improved violations on $1$ of $6$ circuits while degrading HPWL
on every other circuit.  We attribute this to extreme data scarcity (8
ISPD2005 circuits) and conjecture that a learned operator at this scale
must already \emph{contain} the inductive biases (pairwise overlap-driven
repulsion, edge-local wirelength attraction, selective preservation) that the
hand-crafted operator encodes by construction.  Detailed per-circuit
evaluations: \texttt{results/neural\_vsr/eval\_*.json}.

\section{Memory profile of the diffusion backbone}
\label{sec:supp-mem}

In addition to the consolidated table in the main paper, the per-precision
profile reveals two facts about the OOM frontier:

\begin{itemize}
\item Forward-step memory of the attention-GNN scales roughly linearly with
$N$ (macros): $221$\,MB on adaptec2 ($N{=}566$), $1030$\,MB on bigblue4
($N{=}8170$), $2946$\,MB on bigblue2 ($N{=}23084$).  This suggests the
attention key/query/value tensors, not message passing, are the bottleneck.
\item Full-rollout memory (100 denoising steps) saturates near $78$\,GB
on the OOM circuits, consistent with KV-cache-like activation accumulation
across the rollout that flash-attention does not fully release.
\end{itemize}

A natural future direction: replace the backbone's quadratic attention with
linear attention (Performer-style kernelized attention) on the macro-graph
axis; this would unblock bigblue2/4 evaluation but requires backbone
retraining, which is beyond our scope.

Raw rows: \texttt{results/ispd2005/mem\_profile.json} (9 rows: 3 circuits
$\times$ 3 dtypes).
