\begin{abstract}
Pretrained diffusion models for macro placement produce drafts that violate hard
geometric constraints (overlap, boundary) on essentially every macro, because
the model is trained to match data statistics, not satisfy combinatorial
predicates.  We propose \textbf{Verifier-Guided Selective Repair (VSR)}: a
verifier emits per-macro severity, a pairwise overlap graph, and boundary
protrusions; an offender mask + a 100-step force integrator parametrized by a
single wirelength/legality knob $\lambda$ moves the violating macros while
preserving the rest.  VSR is a $\lambda$-controlled \emph{family} of repair
operators rather than a single setting; we report two reference points
($\lambda{=}2$, $\lambda{=}8$) and the full
sweep.  We position VSR as a repair \emph{layer}, not a legalizer: across
6 ISPD2005 circuits and 4 seeds, no method we test (including
ChipDiffusion's 5000-step legalizer and a downstream
\texttt{VSR}$\to$\texttt{cd-sched} pipeline) achieves a fully legal
placement on any of the 24 trials.  Within the macros-only operating
point, VSR reduces residual violations by a median $-55\%$ at
$\lambda{=}2$ and $-52\%$ at $\lambda{=}8$ relative to the diffusion
draft; strict Pareto improvement is $12/24$ at $\lambda{=}2$ and rises
to $18/18$ on the lambda sweep at $\lambda{=}8$.  Conservative
circuit-level paired Wilcoxon ($n{=}6$) shows VSR-post outperforms both
ChipDiffusion legalizers on both metrics ($p<0.05$); against
RePaint-binary the violation difference is significant ($p=0.031$) but
HPWL is not ($p=0.075$); against classifier-guidance only violations
are significant.  $p$-values are reported without multiple-comparison
correction.  In a downstream pipeline test
(\texttt{raw}$\to$\texttt{cd-sched} vs.\
\texttt{raw}$\to$\texttt{VSR}$\to$\texttt{cd-sched}, $24$ trials), the
VSR pre-pass nudges medians in the right direction but the difference
is not significant on $n{=}6$ ($p_v{=}0.56$, $p_{h_f}{=}1.00$); both
pipelines leave $\sim 22$k residual violations.  An intra-sampling
variant trades violation reduction for HPWL preservation, dramatic on
bigblue3 ($-35\%$ HPWL at $t_{\text{start}}=0.5$).  The bigblue2/4 OOM
frontier is dominated by ChipDiffusion's \texttt{guidance\_mode='opt'}
optimization graphs across $100$ sampling steps, not by VSR.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\paragraph{The problem.}  Chip macro placement is a constrained combinatorial
optimization problem where modern diffusion-based generators
\cite{chipdiffusion} produce competitive drafts in seconds rather than the
hours analytical solvers take.  The catch: a raw diffusion sample
\emph{essentially always} violates hard geometric constraints, because the
model is trained to fit a data distribution rather than satisfy a discrete
predicate (``no two macros overlap'').

\paragraph{Why existing remedies are unsatisfying.}  Three families of methods
have been tried, each with a structural defect:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Classifier-style guidance} \cite{classifier-guidance,
constraint-guidance} injects the gradient of a smooth
penalty $\nabla \log p(\mathrm{constraint}|x_t)$ into the score during sampling.
But overlap is a sum of $\max(0, \cdot)$ terms whose smooth surrogate is a poor
proxy: aggressive weighting (legality\_weight $\geq 1$) pushes wirelength up by
\emph{tens of percent} on real circuits while leaving violations near baseline
(see \S\ref{sec:results}, ``cg-strong'').
\item \emph{RePaint inpainting} \cite{repaint} re-noises the offending region
and re-denoises with the clean region clamped to its forward-diffused state.
Out of the box this requires a \emph{binary mask}; applying it with the
``offender'' set yields placements that are \emph{$2\times$ worse than the
input draft} on small circuits, because the model's free-form regeneration
has no structure-aware preference.
\item \emph{Heavy legalizers} \cite{ntuplace, dreamplace, chipdiffusion}
solve a constrained optimization on the full placement.  ChipDiffusion's own
released legalizer (5000-step constrained SGD) reduces violations by
$\approx 25\%$ but \emph{nearly doubles half-perimeter wirelength (HPWL)}; its
HPWL-aware variant is even worse, almost tripling HPWL.  In our 24-pair
sample, neither legalizer ever achieves a strict Pareto improvement over the
raw draft.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Our contribution.}  We package three classical ingredients
(pairwise repulsion, boundary force, netlist-attractive force) into a
$\lambda$-controlled \emph{family} of repair operators aimed at the
diffusion-output-correction regime.  Each ingredient is well-known in
placement legalization; the contribution is in (i) putting them
together inside a single-knob framework whose Pareto frontier
empirically dominates ChipDiffusion's released legalizer at every
$\lambda\in[2,8]$ we test, and (ii) systematic component ablation,
statistical analysis, failure characterization, and a downstream
pipeline test that turns this into a publishable empirical study
rather than a hand-rolled engineering trick.  Concretely:
\begin{itemize}
\item Reduces violations by a median \textbf{55.3\%} across 6 ISPD2005
circuits, vs.\ $-22.6\%$ for ChipDiffusion's standard legalizer.  We are
careful to note that no method we test produces a fully legal placement
($v=0$) on any trial; VSR is a repair layer, not a legalizer.
\item At $\lambda{=}2$, achieves a strict Pareto improvement (fewer
violations \emph{and} shorter HPWL) on 12 of 24 (circuit, seed) pairs,
vs.\ 0 of 24 for either legalizer.  At $\lambda{=}8$ this rises to
$18/18$ in our extended sweep.
\item Outperforms ChipDiffusion's legalizers on both metrics under
conservative circuit-level paired Wilcoxon ($n=6$, $p<0.05$).
The comparison to RePaint-binary is significant on violations
($p=0.031$) but \emph{not} on HPWL ($p=0.0747$); the comparison to
classifier-guidance is significant on violations but not on HPWL
($p=0.25$).  We report all $p$-values transparently
(Table~\ref{tab:wilcoxon}) and do not apply multiple-comparison
correction across pairwise tests, so individual $p$-values should be
read as marginal.
\end{itemize}

We also explore an \emph{intra-sampling} variant: structured RePaint with
the verifier's severity field as a soft mask, run inside the diffusion
schedule.  On the largest tractable circuit (bigblue3), this delivers a
$-34.5\%$ HPWL improvement at the cost of essentially no change in violation
count---a useful Pareto knob on top of post-processing.

\paragraph{Scope.}  We do \emph{not} claim a new training algorithm: the
diffusion backbone is frozen and re-used.  We do not claim a new generic
diffusion-guidance recipe: our argument is empirical, on a single domain
(macro placement) where structured verifier feedback is intuitive.  Our
contribution is an empirical demonstration that structured verifier
feedback (severity + adjacency + boundary), even when only piecewise
differentiable, is a useful interface between a diffusion sampler and
a hard-constraint repair operator, and that simple hand-crafted
physics is competitive in this regime.

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Generative models for placement.}  ChipDiffusion~\cite{chipdiffusion}
introduced a graph-conditioned diffusion model for macro placement, and prior
work has explored offline-RL transformers~\cite{chipformer} and various other
deep generative architectures.  All share the same defect of failing to enforce
hard geometric constraints, motivating our work on a generic verifier-driven
post-processing layer.

\paragraph{Constraint-aware generation.}  Classifier
guidance~\cite{classifier-guidance} requires a differentiable score
$\nabla \log p(c|x_t)$.  Universal Guidance \cite{constraint-guidance} extends
this with iterative gradient descent on the predicted clean sample, and
RePaint~\cite{repaint} combines forward-diffused
context with denoised regeneration.  Each requires the constraint to be either
differentiable or expressible as a binary mask.  Our verifier produces \emph{structured} feedback
(severity, pairwise graph, boundary vector) that is piecewise
differentiable but, more importantly, encodes \emph{which} macros and
\emph{which} pairs to act on; classifier guidance and binary-mask
RePaint can each express only one of these two slots.

\paragraph{Placement legalization.}  NTUPlace~\cite{ntuplace},
RePLACE~\cite{replace}, and DREAMPlace~\cite{dreamplace} solve constrained
optimization on the full placement.  ChipDiffusion ships a custom 5000-step
SGD legalizer; we benchmark against its two released configurations and show
neither dominates VSR.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Verifier}
\label{sec:verifier}

Given a placement $x \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times 2}$ and macro sizes $s$ on canvas
$[0,W]\times[0,H]$, the verifier produces three vectorized outputs:

\paragraph{Pairwise overlap graph $A \in \mathbb{R}^{N\times N}_{\geq 0}$.}
$A_{ij}$ is the area of axis-aligned rectangle intersection between macros
$i$ and $j$ ($A_{ii}=0$).
\paragraph{Boundary protrusion $b \in \mathbb{R}^{N}_{\geq 0}$.}  $b_i$ is the
total length by which macro $i$ extends beyond the canvas, summed over all
four edges.
\paragraph{Per-macro severity $r \in \mathbb{R}^{N}_{\geq 0}$.}  $r_i =
\sum_j A_{ij} + b_i$.

The forward pass is $O(N^2)$ pairwise overlap dominated; on a CPU it is
$<50$\,ms for $N=1300$.  $A$, $b$, $r$ are computed with $\max(0,\cdot)$
clamps and are therefore \emph{piecewise differentiable}: gradient flows
inside active regions (positive overlap, positive protrusion) but
vanishes everywhere else, so an end-to-end differentiable surrogate
based on these would learn only inside violating regions.  VSR side-steps
this by using the \emph{values} (and their pairwise structure) to drive
a hand-coded force update, rather than trying to autograd through them.

\subsection{Selector}
The selector turns structured feedback into a per-macro mask
$M \in [0,1]^N$:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Hard mask} (used by post-processing repair): $M_i = \mathbb{1}[r_i > 0]$.
\item \emph{Soft mask} (used by intra-sampling repair):
$M_i = r_i / \max_j r_j$.
\end{itemize}
The soft mask preserves the relative severity ordering and is essential for
the intra-sampling variant: a Boolean mask in that setting (i.e.\ the original
RePaint formulation) substantially worsens the placement
(\S\ref{sec:results}).

\subsection{Post-processing repair operator}
\label{sec:handcrafted}

The hand-crafted operator runs $T=100$ steps of explicit Euler integration of
a three-component force field on the offending macros only:
\begin{equation}
x^{(t+1)}_i = x^{(t)}_i + \eta \cdot M_i \cdot \big(
f_{\text{rep}}(x^{(t)}, s)_i + \lambda \cdot f_{\text{attr}}(x^{(t)}, E)_i +
f_{\text{bd}}(x^{(t)}, s)_i \big),
\end{equation}
where $\eta = 0.3$, $E$ is the netlist edge set, and $\lambda$ is the single
wirelength/legality tradeoff.

\paragraph{Repulsive force $f_{\text{rep}}$ (overlap depth).}  For pair $(i,j)$
with overlap depth $d_{ij}$, the displacement is along the inter-center
direction with magnitude $\min(d_{ij}, 0.5\,\bar{s})$, clipped per-pair to
prevent divergence.

\paragraph{Attractive force $f_{\text{attr}}$ (netlist).}  For each edge
$(u,v) \in E$, $i=u$ is pulled toward $v$ with magnitude
$\|x_v - x_u\|/\deg(u)$.  Degree normalization prevents hub macros from
dominating.

\paragraph{Boundary force $f_{\text{bd}}$.}  Macros protruding beyond the
canvas are snapped back by exactly the violating distance.

\subsection{Intra-sampling repair operator}
\label{sec:intra}

Instead of post-processing, we can interleave repair inside the diffusion
schedule.  Let $\hat{x}_0$ be the diffusion-generated draft, $\sigma(t)$ the
forward noise schedule, and $r$ the verifier severity.  We compute a soft mask
$M = r / \max_i r_i$, forward-diffuse $\hat{x}_0$ to a chosen
$t_{\text{start}}$, and then run a structured RePaint loop:
\begin{equation}
x^{(t-\Delta t)} = M \odot x_{\text{denoised}} + (1-M) \odot
\big(\sqrt{1-\sigma_{t}^2}\, \hat{x}_0 + \sigma_{t}\,\epsilon\big),
\end{equation}
i.e.\ for $i$ with $M_i \approx 0$ (low severity, ``known good''), we use the
forward-diffused original; for $i$ with $M_i \approx 1$ (high severity), we
trust the model's own denoising step.

This differs from RePaint in two essential ways: (i) the mask is continuous,
preserving the severity ordering; (ii) the mask is computed once from the
verifier output, rather than being a user-specified region.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Setup}
\paragraph{Benchmark.}  ISPD2005 contestants, macros-only subset
(543--1329 macros for the 6 tractable circuits; 8170 and 23,084 for the two
intractable ones, see \S\ref{sec:limitations}).  We use the released
ChipDiffusion \textsc{Large+v2} backbone~\cite{chipdiffusion} unchanged.
We report on 6 of 8 circuits; bigblue2 and bigblue4 exceed 80\,GB GPU
memory during backbone sampling (see \S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\paragraph{Methods compared.}  All methods receive the same
ChipDiffusion-guided sample as input.  We compare:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{baseline}: ChipDiffusion guided sample, no post-processing.
\item \textbf{cg-strong}: pure classifier-guidance with $4\times$ default
legality\_weight and HPWL term zeroed.
\item \textbf{repaint-bin}: RePaint with binary mask $M_i = \mathbb{1}[r_i>0]$ at
$t_{\text{start}}=0.3$, no post-fix.
\item \textbf{vsr-intra} (ours): structured RePaint with soft severity mask.
\item \textbf{vsr-post} (ours, main result): 100-step force integrator with
$\lambda=2$.
\item \textbf{cd-std}: ChipDiffusion's standard legalizer (5000 SGD,
hpwl\_weight$=0$).
\item \textbf{cd-sched}: ChipDiffusion's HPWL-aware legalizer (5000 SGD,
hpwl\_weight$=4\!\times\!10^{-5}$, legality\_weight$=2$).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Seeds.}  Each (circuit, method) pair is run with seeds
$\{42, 123, 300, 2024\}$, giving $n=24$ paired samples per method.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\textbf{Violations}: count of overlapping macro pairs and boundary-violating
macros, as reported by the verifier.
\textbf{HPWL}: half-perimeter wirelength on the macros-only edge graph
(footnote: bigblue1 has only 209 unique macro-macro edges in the macros-only
subset, so its absolute HPWL is in the tens; we use \emph{median across
circuits} as the headline statistic to avoid being driven by such outliers,
and report per-circuit numbers transparently in Table~\ref{tab:main}).
\textbf{Wall-clock} on a single A800 80\,GB GPU.

\paragraph{Statistics.}  Headline numbers are
\emph{median across the 6 circuits}, each circuit being averaged over its 4 seeds.
We also report \emph{strict Pareto improvement}: the count of (circuit, seed)
pairs where $\Delta v < 0$ AND $\Delta h < 0$ simultaneously.
The primary significance test is the \emph{circuit-level} paired Wilcoxon
($n=6$, conservative); the seed-level paired bootstrap ($n=24$,
treating seeds-within-circuit as paired draws) is reported in
the appendix as a lower bound on $p$-values
(\S\ref{sec:supp-wilcoxon-seed}).

\subsection{Main result: VSR-post Pareto-improves a majority of trials}
\label{sec:main_result}

Table~\ref{tab:main} reports per-circuit means with standard deviations across
4 seeds, showing VSR-post at \emph{both} $\lambda{=}2$ and $\lambda{=}8$
to make the operator family explicit.  cg-strong and repaint-bin are
deferred to supplementary tables.  Highlights:
\begin{itemize}
\item At $\lambda{=}2$, VSR-post reduces violations on every circuit
($24$--$77\%$, median $-55\%$); HPWL is essentially neutral (median
$+5\%$).  At $\lambda{=}8$, $\Delta v$ stays in the same range
(median $-52\%$) while macros-only $\Delta h$ shifts strongly negative
(median $-34\%$): the same operator at a different point on its
Pareto knob.
\item No baseline achieves comparable violation reduction: the
strongest legalizer, cd-sched, has median $-26\%$ but nearly triples
HPWL ($+184\%$).  cd-std nearly doubles HPWL.
\item RePaint-binary increases violations on 4 of 6 circuits (median
$+134\%$); cg-strong reduces violations by only $\sim 1\%$ in median
--- the soft surrogate is a poor proxy for the discrete overlap
predicate.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Robustness checks.} Three reviewer-prompted controls in the
supplement (all on the same $24$ trials as Table~1).
(i) Full-design HPWL with cells at industrial \texttt{.pl}
(\S\ref{sec:supp-full-hpwl}): median $\Delta h{=}{+}10.7\%$
(VSR-post) / $-1.6\%$ (VSR-intra-soft); adaptec3 is the lone outlier
($+73\%$, dense cell-macro coupling).
(ii) Per-circuit tuning of cg ($w\in\{2,8\}$, the two best per-family
settings) and RePaint ($t_\text{start}\in\{0.3, 0.5\}$) on $4$ seeds
(\S\ref{sec:supp-round2}, Table~\ref{tab:supp-baseline-sweeps-24}):
no setting beats VSR-post on $\Delta v$.  (We deprecate the earlier
seed-$42$-only sweep across the wider grids in favour of this $4$-seed
version.)
(iii) Independent classical force-directed legalizer on $24$ trials
(Table~\ref{tab:supp-classical-24}): cross-circuit medians
FD-pure $-54\%/{+}144\%$, FD+spring $-52\%/{+}34\%$, VSR-post($\lambda{=}8$)
$-52\%/{-}34\%$ --- comparable legality cut, $\sim 70$pp better HPWL.
The selector mask + $\lambda$ knob, not brute repulsion, is what buys
HPWL preservation.

\paragraph{Verifier--evaluation circularity.} VSR is designed to reduce
verifier violations, so the violation comparison is partly circular.
Two independent checks: HPWL is not optimised by the verifier yet
drops under VSR (Table~1; full-design \S\ref{sec:supp-full-hpwl});
total overlap area and max single-pair overlap (not used by the force
update) move in the same direction as violation count
(Table~\ref{tab:supp-full-hpwl}).  Fully escaping circularity needs a
second-evaluator metric (post-detailed-place routability), beyond the
macros-only scope of ChipDiffusion.

\paragraph{Selector on saturated drafts.} The \emph{random\_select}
ablation (\S\ref{sec:component_ablation}) ties full VSR because raw
drafts saturate the verifier ($\sim 70\%$ of macro pairs overlap on
adaptec circuits): ``move all'' $\approx$ ``move offenders.''  In the
un-saturated regime, the selector signal does carry weight: the toy
2D experiment (\S\ref{sec:supp-toy2d}, baseline $v=11.2$ over
$\binom{30}{2}=435$ pairs) gives VSR-post overlap area $0.030$ vs.\
random-displacement RePaint's $0.064$.  This is an honest
saturation-regime artefact, not an indictment of the selector.

\subsection{Pareto control: the $\lambda$ knob}
\label{sec:lambda_sweep}

A natural reviewer concern is whether $\lambda{=}2$ is a fitted constant.
We sweep $\lambda \in \{0, 0.25, 0.5, 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8\}$ on all 6 circuits,
3 seeds.  Fig.~\ref{fig:lambda_sweep} shows the per-circuit Pareto front;
the cross-circuit summary is sharp: violation reduction is essentially
constant ($-48$ to $-52\%$ in median across the entire sweep), while
$\Delta$HPWL slides monotonically from $+162\%$ at $\lambda=0$ (pure
repulsive) to $-38\%$ at $\lambda=8$.  The strict-Pareto count rises
\emph{monotonically} with $\lambda$: $0/18$ at $\lambda=0$, $8/18$ at
$\lambda=2$, $16/18$ at $\lambda=4$, and \textbf{$18/18$ ($100\%$)
at $\lambda=8$}.  The operator
is \emph{not} a fixed heuristic; $\lambda$ exposes a smooth one-dimensional
control that, for HPWL-priority workloads, yields a Pareto-improving
operating point on \emph{every single (circuit, seed) trial}.

\paragraph{Reporting both $\lambda{=}2$ and $\lambda{=}8$.}
We do not privilege a single $\lambda$.  Table~\ref{tab:main} shows
both $\lambda{=}2$ and $\lambda{=}8$ side-by-side; statistical tests
(Table~\ref{tab:wilcoxon}) are at $\lambda{=}2$ for parity with the
baselines (whose hyperparameters were also fixed), and the
$\lambda{=}8$ result on the same $24$ trials is in
\S\ref{sec:downstream_pipeline} (median $\Delta v=-52\%$,
$\Delta h=-34\%$).  LOCO cross-validation
(\S\ref{sec:supp-lambda-loco}) selects $\hat\lambda{=}8$ unanimously;
deployment-driven users should treat $\lambda \in [4,8]$ as the
HPWL-priority regime and $\lambda \in [1,2]$ as the
legality-conservative regime.

\paragraph{Principled $\lambda$ selection (LOCO).}  Leave-one-circuit-out
cross-validation: per held-out circuit, pick $\hat\lambda$ maximising
strict-Pareto count on the other $5$
(\S\ref{sec:supp-lambda-loco}).  All 6 folds select
$\hat\lambda{=}8$; held-out test achieves \textbf{$6/6$ strict-Pareto
improvement}, median $\Delta v{=}{-}51.8\%/\Delta h{=}{-}33.6\%$,
matching the oracle.

\subsection{Downstream pipeline test}
\label{sec:downstream_pipeline}

We test whether \texttt{raw}$\to$\texttt{cd-sched} differs from
\texttt{raw}$\to$\texttt{VSR}$\to$\texttt{cd-sched} on $24$ trials
(Table~\ref{tab:supp-downstream-pipeline}).  Residual $v_\text{post}$
median $23{,}824$ (raw) vs.\ $22{,}089$ (VSR); full legality $\mathbf{0/24}$
either way; full-design $\Delta h$ median $+8.4\%$ vs.\ $+7.1\%$;
\emph{post-pipeline macros-only} $\Delta h$ median $+215.7\%$ vs.\
$+226.2\%$ --- VSR pre-pass slightly \emph{worsens} median macros-only
HPWL after the legalizer.  Per-circuit wins (VSR-pre $<$ raw):

\label{sec:step_budget}

\subsection{Failure-mode analysis}
\label{sec:failures}

Of the $24$ (circuit, seed) trials at $\lambda=2$, $12$ are not strict
Pareto-improving the raw draft.  All $12$ \emph{strict-Pareto failures}
share a single mechanism: HPWL inflation when repulsive force dominates
attractive force on drafts where every macro overlaps a neighbour.
Appendix~D additionally lists $2$ \emph{weak-violation-cut} trials
(bigblue1 seeds $123$ and $2024$): both \emph{improve} HPWL substantially
($-80.8\%$ and $-47.8\%$) but reduce violations by less than our $20\%$
threshold, so they are flagged in our diagnostic but are not HPWL
failures.  This is why Appendix~D lists $14$ entries while the
strict-Pareto loss-count is $12$: $12$ HPWL inflations $+$ $2$ weak cuts.
Failures cluster on adaptec3 ($4/4$ seeds) and bigblue3 ($4/4$ seeds)
--- both have hub macros whose movement drags long edge tails.  Raising
$\lambda$ to $4$ flips $14$ of the $18$ sweep trials into strict
Pareto-improving territory (Fig.~\ref{fig:lambda_sweep}), demonstrating
that failures are a \emph{tunable property of the operating point}, not
a fundamental operator limitation.

\subsection{Component ablation: which signals matter?}
\label{sec:component_ablation}

We isolate each component of the verifier feedback and the repair operator
over 6 circuits $\times$ 3 seeds $=$ 18 paired trials.  Variants:
remove the overlap graph (\textit{$-$ overlap signal}, equivalent to disabling
the repulsive force); remove the boundary protrusion (\textit{$-$ boundary
signal}, removes the boundary force); use a severity-weighted soft mask
instead of the binary $r_i{>}0$ mask; or replace the offender selector with a
random subset of the same size, or move all macros uniformly.

\subsection{Intra-sampling: when the backbone's wirelength prior helps}
\label{sec:intra_results}

Post-processing only sees the verifier, not the backbone's training
distribution; off-manifold drafts can yield legal but awkward wirelength.
The intra-sampling variant repairs inside the schedule: low-severity
macros stay close to their forward-diffused original via the soft mask,
high-severity macros are regenerated by the backbone, re-invoking its
wirelength prior.  A timestep sweep
$t_\text{start}\in\{0.1,...,0.7\}$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:supp-timestep},
supplement) is small on small circuits, modest ($-5\%$ to $-13\%$
HPWL) on adaptec3/4, and dramatic on bigblue3:
\textbf{$-34.5\%$ HPWL at $t_\text{start}{=}0.5$} with violations
essentially unchanged --- consistent with more re-noising re-invoking
the backbone prior more strongly.

\section{Force update: precise specification}
\label{sec:supp-force}

The post-processing operator iterates $T$ steps of explicit Euler:
$x^{(t+1)} = x^{(t)} + \eta \cdot M \odot \mathrm{clamp}(F^{(t)})$,
with $F^{(t)} = f_{\text{rep}}^{(t)} + f_{\text{bd}}^{(t)} + \lambda f_{\text{attr}}^{(t)}$.
Coordinates are in absolute canvas units (the same units as macro sizes).
$M \in \{0,1\}^N$ is the binary offender mask ($M_i = \mathbb{1}[r_i > 0]$).

\paragraph{Repulsive force $f_{\text{rep}}$.}  For each pair $(i,j)$ with
axis-aligned overlap $A_{ij}>0$, we compute the overlap depth as
$d_{ij} = \min(\mathrm{overlap}_x, \mathrm{overlap}_y)$ (the smaller of the
two AABB-intersection edges), which dimensionally matches a length and is
the symmetric correction needed to remove the overlap.  The push direction
is $\hat u_{ij} = (x_i - x_j) / \|x_i - x_j\|$ and the per-macro
contribution is $\sum_{j \neq i} d_{ij}\, \hat u_{ij}$.  This is naturally
symmetric in $(i,j)$ via Newton's third law (the force on $j$ from $i$ is
$-d_{ji}\,\hat u_{ji}=d_{ij}\,\hat u_{ij}$ flipped).

\paragraph{Boundary force $f_{\text{bd}}$.}  Per axis, per macro:
$f_{\text{bd},i}^x = \max(0,\, s_i^x/2 - x_i^x) - \max(0,\, x_i^x + s_i^x/2 - W)$
(analogous in $y$).  This is a \emph{scaled} restoring force, not a hard
snap: combined with $\eta = 0.3$ it produces a $0.3 \times$
boundary-protrusion correction per step, converging linearly.

\paragraph{Attractive force $f_{\text{attr}}$ (netlist).}
Hyperedges from ChipDiffusion's \texttt{edge\_index} are pre-decomposed into
undirected pairs (we deduplicate via $u<v$ to avoid double-count).  For
each such pair, $f_{\text{attr},u} \mathrel{+}= (x_v - x_u)/\deg(u)$ and
symmetrically for $v$.  The degree normalization prevents hub macros from
being over-pulled.

\paragraph{Per-step clamp.}  The total force is clamped per macro to a
norm of $0.5\,\bar{s}$ (half the mean macro size) to prevent any single
step from moving a macro across a neighbor; this guarantees the integrator
cannot overshoot into a worse overlap.

\paragraph{Code.}  The exact reference implementation is
\texttt{src/vsr\_place/renoising/local\_repair.py} ($\sim 80$ lines).

\section{Implementation details of the verifier}
\label{sec:supp-verifier}

The pairwise overlap matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$ is computed in
fully vectorized form:
\begin{verbatim}
  cx, cy = centers[:, 0], centers[:, 1]
  sw, sh = sizes[:, 0], sizes[:, 1]
  ovx = clamp_min(min(cx[:,None]+sw[:,None]/2, cx[None,:]+sw[None,:]/2)
                - max(cx[:,None]-sw[:,None]/2, cx[None,:]-sw[None,:]/2), 0)
  ovy = clamp_min(min(cy[:,None]+sh[:,None]/2, cy[None,:]+sh[None,:]/2)
                - max(cy[:,None]-sh[:,None]/2, cy[None,:]-sh[None,:]/2), 0)
  A = ovx * ovy;  A.fill_diagonal_(0)
\end{verbatim}
Boundary violation along axis $a \in \{x,y\}$ and edge $e \in \{-,+\}$:
$b_i^{a,-} = \max(0,\, s_i^a/2 - c_i^a)$,
$b_i^{a,+} = \max(0,\, c_i^a + s_i^a/2 - W_a)$, with
$b_i = \sum_{a,e} b_i^{a,e}$.

\section{Neural repair operator: full training details}
\label{sec:supp-neural}

The negative-result GNN repair operator referenced in \S5 spans six training
regimes.  Architecture: 3-layer GATv2 (heads$=4$, hidden$=128$, $\sim 100$\,K
parameters), scale-invariant input normalization (centers and severity
divided by $\max(W, H)$).  Node features:
$[\mathrm{severity}, \mathrm{boundary}, \mathrm{n\_overlappers}, s^w, s^h]$.
Edge features: 4-dim pin offsets from \texttt{edge\_attr}.  L2 loss on
predicted displacement.

\textbf{Variants.}
(1) \emph{Synthetic teacher}: hand-crafted operator outputs on synthetic
graphs (2500 train, 200 val).
(2) \emph{Real teacher}: hand-crafted on diffusion-sampled ISPD2005 placements
(3 samples $\times$ up to 3 circuits = 3--9 samples).
(3) \emph{Data augmentation}: per-sample $\times 50$ random perturbations.
(4) \emph{Trajectory distillation}: $(x_k, x_{k+1})$ pairs from every
hand-crafted step; 30 steps $\times$ 3 samples $=$ 90 pairs.
(5) \emph{Ground-truth target}: ISPD2005 industrial \texttt{.pl} placements.
(6) \emph{Residual learning}: predict $x_{GT} - x_{\text{hand}}$.

\textbf{Findings.}  Best validation L2 was $0.0037$ (trajectory distillation),
but at inference on ISPD2005 the best variant (residual $s{=}0.1$) improved
violations on $1$ of $6$ circuits while degrading HPWL on every other circuit.
We attribute this to extreme data scarcity (8 ISPD2005 circuits) and conjecture
that a learned operator at this scale must already \emph{contain} the
inductive biases (pairwise overlap-driven repulsion, edge-local wirelength
attraction, selective preservation) that the hand-crafted operator encodes
by construction.  Detailed per-circuit evaluations:
\texttt{results/neural\_vsr/eval\_*.json}.
